# 1 - What is light?

This is a deceptively simple question. You flip a switch, the room lights up, and you see. Light is so woven into everyday experience that most people never stop to ask what it actually _is_. But the moment you try to answer, you find yourself pulled in different directions. Watch a sunbeam cut through dust and it looks like a stream of particles flying in a straight line. Pass it through a narrow slit and it spreads out and bends around corners, the way water waves do in a harbor. Shine it on the right metal surface and electrons fly out, one photon at a time. Which picture is correct?

Humanity has been wrestling with this question for thousands of years, and the history of that struggle is the history of optics itself. We will begin this chapter by tracing some of the key milestones, from polished crystals in ancient Assyria to the Nobel Prize-winning super-resolution techniques of the 21st century. Many of the names and ideas you will encounter here will reappear throughout the book, and understanding the historical context will make them easier to remember and harder to confuse.

We will then look at the three models that physicists use to describe light. As a **wave**, light has a wavelength, a frequency and an amplitude; it can interfere and diffract, and these phenomena set the fundamental limit to what a microscope can resolve. As a **ray**, light travels in straight lines and bends at interfaces between different materials; this is the framework we use to understand how lenses form images and how aberrations degrade them. As a **particle**, light comes in discrete packets of energy called photons; this is the language of fluorescence, absorption and emission, and it is essential for understanding how modern microscopy detects single molecules.

If you are the kind of person who wants a clean, definitive answer to the question, this section may irritate you. That is rather the point.

At the end of this chapter you should be able to:
- Recognize how key historical milestones led to the development of modern optical microscopy, from early lens-making to super-resolution techniques
- Describe light as an electromagnetic wave and define its wavelength, frequency, amplitude and speed
- Define the refractive index of a medium and calculate how wavelength changes when light enters a material
- Apply Snell's law to predict the direction of a refracted ray at an interface
- Use the thin lens equation and ray tracing rules to locate the image formed by a converging lens
- Relate the energy of a photon to its frequency and wavelength
- Distinguish between absorption, spontaneous emission and stimulated emission
- Argue the nature of light
