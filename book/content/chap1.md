# 1 - What is light?

It is a deceptively simple question. You flip a switch, the room lights up, and you see. Light is so woven into everyday experience that most people never stop to ask what it actually _is_. But the moment you try to answer, you find yourself pulled in different directions. Watch a sunbeam cut through dust and it looks like a stream of particles flying in a straight line. Look at a soap bubble and the swirling colors tell you it must be a wave. Shine it on the right metal surface and electrons fly out, one photon at a time. Which picture is correct?

Humanity has been wrestling with this question for well over two thousand years, and the history of that struggle is the history of optics itself. We will begin this chapter by tracing some of the key milestones, from polished crystals in ancient Assyria to the Nobel Prize-winning super-resolution techniques of the 21st century. This is not just a timeline to memorize: many of the names and ideas you will encounter here will reappear throughout the book, and understanding the historical context will make them easier to remember and harder to confuse.

We will then look at the three models that physicists use to describe light. As a **wave**, light has a wavelength, a frequency and an amplitude; it can interfere and diffract, and these phenomena set the fundamental limit to what a microscope can resolve. As a **ray**, light travels in straight lines and bends at interfaces between different materials; this is the framework we use to understand how lenses form images and how aberrations degrade them. As a **particle**, light comes in discrete packets of energy called photons; this is the language of fluorescence, absorption and emission, and it is essential for understanding how modern microscopy detects single molecules.

Each of these descriptions is incomplete on its own, and none of them is "the right one." The chapter ends with a short reflection on what this means, not just for physics, but for how we think about scientific models in general. If you are the kind of person who wants a clean, definitive answer, this section may irritate you. That is rather the point.

At the end of this chapter you should be able to:
- Trace the key historical milestones that led to the development of modern optical microscopy, from early lens-making to super-resolution techniques
- Describe light as an electromagnetic wave and define its wavelength, frequency, amplitude and speed
- Explain constructive and destructive interference and describe how diffraction at a finite aperture produces an Airy pattern
- Define the refractive index of a medium and calculate how wavelength changes when light enters a material
- Apply Snell's law to predict the direction of a refracted ray at an interface
- Use the thin lens equation and ray tracing rules to locate the image formed by a converging lens
- Relate the energy of a photon to its frequency and wavelength using $E = h\nu$
- Distinguish between absorption, spontaneous emission and stimulated emission and identify the role of each in microscopy
- Describe the Stokes shift and explain why fluorescence emission occurs at a longer wavelength than excitation
- Argue why no single model (wave, ray or particle) fully describes the nature of light and discuss the implications for how we use models in science
